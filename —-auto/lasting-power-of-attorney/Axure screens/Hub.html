Adding extra information

Restrictions and conditions

 

You can choose to specify certain things that the attorneys must or must not do. These are called restrictions and conditions. For example you could specify certain procedures they must follow, situations where they must seek professional advice or things they must not do. You do not have to specify any restrictions and conditions and, if you do not, then your attorneys will be able to make all decisions on your behalf. The attorneys must follow the restrictions and conditions.

 

Guidance to your attorneys

 

You can choose to specify certain information that you would like the attorneys to consider when making decisions. This is called guidance. You do not have to specify any guidance. The attorneys are not obliged to follow the guidance but it should help them understand the donors wishes when making decisions.

 

Charges and expenses

 

Professional attorneys, such as solicitors, charge for their services. Other attorneys can only be paid for their time if the donor has agreed to pay them a fee. The donor must specify the amount of the fee in the LPA. The donor can choose to pay different amounts to different attorneys. All attorneys can claim reasonable out of pocket expenses for performing their duties. This covers things like stationery, postage etc.

Specifying how attorneys can make decisions

If you appoint more than one attorney you can choose whether they act together or on their own. The three choices are:

 

Jointly: Attorneys must always make all decisions together. All attorneys must agree all decisions and sign any relevant documents.

 

Jointly and severally: Attorneys can make decisions together but can also make decisions independently. Any action taken by any attorney alone is as valid as if they were the only attorney.

 

Jointly for some decisions and jointly and severally for other decisions: Attorneys must make certain specified decisions together but they may also make certain other decisions independently. You decide which decisions must be made by all attorneys together. For example, you may wish your attorneys to act individually on most financial matters but to act together if they are selling your house.

Certificate provider

A certificate provider is an independent person who can confirm that the donor understands the significance of the LPA. They must confirm that the donor has not been put under pressure to make the LPA and that there is no fraud involved. The certificate provider must be someone who has known the donor well for at least two years or else they can be someone who has the skills to know whether the donor understand the significance of the lasting power of attorney (such as a doctor or solicitor).

 

You must choose at least one certificate provider. If you have not chosen any people to be told then you must choose two certificate providers. None of the attorneys can be a certificate provider. Any of the people to be told can be a certificate provider.

People to be told

The people to be told are people who know the donor well and who you choose to be notified when the LPA is registered. They will then have the chance to raise concerns or objections.

 

You can choose up to five people to be told. You do not have to choose any people to be told, but if you do not choose anyone then you will have to choose an extra certificate provider. The people to be told do not have to sign the lasting power of attorney.

Replacement attorneys

A replacement attorney is someone who will take on the role of an attorney if one of the original attorneys becomes unable to perform their duties.

 

You can choose to have one or more replacement attorneys. If you have more than one replacement attorney you will have to decide in which order they will take over from the original attorneys.

The attorney

An attorney is a person the donor chooses to make decisions for them when they cannot make decisions for themselves. It is a very important role, so the donor must choose someone they trust to make decisions for them and who knows them well enough to make decisions in their best interest. You must also make sure the person is comfortable and willing to take on the role.

The word “attorney” means a person appointed to act on behalf of someone in legal matters. It does not mean a solicitor and you do not have to choose a solicitor to be the attorney (you can choose to have a solicitor act as attorney but it is not compulsory to do so).

 

You can choose to have more than one attorney. If you choose more than one attorney you will have to decide whether you want them to make all or some decisions together or whether you want them to make all or some decisions individually. If you make two lasting powers of attorney (one for finance and property and one for health and welfare) you can choose to have the same or different attorneys for each lasting power of attorney.

The Donor

The donor is the person who makes a lasting power of attorney and chooses people to make decisions for them. The purpose of a lasting power of attorney is to make sure decisions are made in the donor’s best interests. It is OK for someone to help the donor complete the lasting power of attorney, or to complete it for them, as long as the decisions in the lasting power of attorney have been made by the donor.
